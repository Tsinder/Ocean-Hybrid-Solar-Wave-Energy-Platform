# Abstract

This document describes a hybrid offshore renewable energy system capable of harvesting energy from multiple environmental sources simultaneously.

The proposed Ocean Hybrid Solar–Wave Energy Platform (OHSWEP) integrates:

- high-efficiency tandem photovoltaic cells  
- solar concentrator optics  
- wave pressure energy harvesting  
- hydrodynamic seawater cooling  
- marine corrosion protection systems  
- modular floating ocean infrastructure  

The architecture is designed to operate in extreme marine environments where structures may be continuously exposed to:

- saltwater  
- waves  
- UV radiation  
- mechanical stress  
- biofouling  

The system can operate as a single autonomous module or be scaled into ocean-scale renewable energy farms.

This document is published publicly to establish prior art and prevent patent monopolization of the disclosed architecture.

# 3 Solar Energy Subsystem

## 3.1 Tandem Photovoltaic Architecture

The system uses tandem photovoltaic cells composed of two absorber layers.

Top layer  
Perovskite photovoltaic absorber

Bottom layer  
Monocrystalline silicon photovoltaic absorber

Advantages:

- broader solar spectrum absorption
- improved photon utilization
- higher conversion efficiency

Expected efficiency:

30–35 %

Future optimized tandem architectures may exceed 40 % efficiency.

- UV-resistant optical polymers

# 5 Wave Energy Harvesting Layer

The lower structural layer converts wave motion into electrical energy.

Three complementary energy harvesting mechanisms may be integrated.

## 5.1 Piezoelectric Harvesting

Wave pressure deforms piezoelectric materials.

Possible materials:

- PVDF polymer
- PZT ceramics

Advantages:

- no rotating mechanical parts
- long operational lifetime
- scalable architecture

---

## 5.2 Hydraulic Micro-Generator System

Wave pressure slightly displaces the floating platform.

This displacement drives hydraulic pistons connected to micro turbines and generators.

Advantages:

- higher energy density
- scalable mechanical amplification

---

## 5.3 Triboelectric Nanogenerators

Water motion across specialized surfaces generates electrical charge through triboelectric interaction.

These systems may provide supplementary energy generation.

# 1 Solar Energy Model

Solar power generation is calculated as:

P = A × G × η

Where:

P = electrical power output  
A = panel surface area  
G = solar irradiance  
η = photovoltaic efficiency

Typical values:

G = 1000 W/m²  
η = 0.30 – 0.35

Example:

10 m² module

P ≈ 3000 W

---

# 2 Solar Concentrator Model

Solar concentrators increase irradiance.

Effective irradiance:

G_eff = C × G

Where:

C = concentration ratio

Typical range:

C = 5 – 20

Thermal cooling is required for stable operation.

---

# 3 Wave Energy Model

Wave energy per unit width is:

P_wave = (ρ g² H² T) / (64π)

Where:

ρ = water density  
g = gravitational acceleration  
H = wave height  
T = wave period

Typical ocean conditions:

H = 1–3 m  
T = 5–10 s

Estimated available power:

5–20 kW per meter of wave front.

---

# 4 Piezoelectric Energy Model

Energy generated by piezoelectric deformation:

E = ½ k x²

Where:

k = material stiffness  
x = deformation displacement

Repeated wave pressure cycles produce electrical output.

---

# 5 Hydraulic Generator Model

Hydraulic energy conversion:

P = ρ g Q H

Where:

ρ = fluid density  
Q = flow rate  
H = hydraulic head

Wave-driven hydraulic pistons create flow through micro turbines.

---

# 6 Total Hybrid Energy Output

Total system energy output:

P_total = P_solar + P_wave + P_piezo + P_hydraulic

Hybrid systems increase total energy density by combining multiple sources.

Estimated improvement:

40–60 % over conventional solar systems.
